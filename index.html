Research interests

Physics Beyond the Standard Model

The Standard Model (SM) of particle physics gives a uni fied theoretical description of phenomena governed by the strong, weak, and electromagnetic interactions. It has been incredibly predictive in a wide range of experiments; yet besides its success it is an e ffective field theory and it is not yet clear at what energy it will require new laws of physics (for instance, it does not include dark matter).

Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) is the theory which describes the strong interaction. The SM involves QCD at di fferent regimes: at high energies (at the mass of the W-bosons MW  ~O(100GeV )) it is possible to perform a perturbative expansion while at the hadronic scale,  ~O(1GeV ), we need to use a non-perturbative approach. Lattice QCD is a non-perturbative de nition of QCD, based on a lattice regularization, which makes the theory well-defi ned at a mathematical level and allows us to compute quantities of interest in the strongly coupled regime, from first principles. It allows one to perform analytical and numerical studies. The signi ficant advantage of this formulation is that one can evaluate the path integral numerically via Monte Carlo methods. Once the lattice theory is de fined and renormalized, the continuum theory is recovered by sending the lattice spacing to zero.

One of the roles of lattice QCD in precision physics is to provide matrix elements for the so called eff ective hamiltonians and parameters such as the strong coupling constant and the quark masses. There are two ways of researching the possible extensions of the SM on the lattice. The first is indirect: doing precision measurements at low energies. For instance, a very precise determination of the parameters which control the flavor mixing will make possible to find small contributions to decay amplitudes coming from new particles. They depend upon the hadronic matrix elements which are non-perturbative and can be measured by means of lattice simulations. The second way of researching the possible extension of the SM on the lattice is direct: it consists in performing simulations with either di erent gauge groups or di fferent representations for the matter fields, as those models can provide viable extensions of the SM.
